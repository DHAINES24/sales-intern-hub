The 7 Basics of a Champion

Prospecting

Don't wait for leads. Actively seek prospects through every channel. Every interaction is an opportunity to build awareness and credibility.

Original Contact

Make a strong first impression — even outside sales settings. Remember details to connect more meaningfully in follow-ups.

Qualification

Filter your time to people who can say "yes." Spending time with unqualified prospects is the fastest way to burn out and underperform.

Presentation

Make them feel like your product was built specifically for them. Tailor every pitch to their specific needs and language.

Handling Objections

Anticipate every objection before it arrives. Respond with confidence, empathy, and clear value. Turn "no" into "not yet — tell me more."

Closing the Sale

The close is the natural conclusion of a well-run process. Use trial closes throughout. Read buying signals. Never fear asking — you're helping them decide.

Referrals

The close of one sale opens the next. A warm referral is exponentially more valuable than any cold prospect. Protect every relationship like gold.

Qualification is where new reps waste the most time. Hammer it: time spent with unqualified prospects is the #1 cause of burnout.

Open Questions — Use WWWWWH

Force the prospect to think — not just give a reflex yes/no. These open the door.

The Framework

  • Who will be using this the most?
  • What feature is most critical for you?
  • Where do you see this evolving?
  • When would you like this implemented?
  • Why are you investing in this now?
  • How do you see it benefiting your team?

Trading Simulator Examples

  • "Who will be using this software most frequently?"
  • "What's the most important feature you need?"
  • "When would you like this implemented by?"
  • "Why are you interested in a simulator now?"
  • "How do you see this benefiting your interns?"

"If I say it, they tend to doubt me. If they say it — it's true."

Open questions get them talking and selling themselves. WWWWWH is the cheat sheet. Closing line is the whole philosophy: let them say it.

The Bond Framework

4 rules that make people want to buy from you specifically

🪞

Mirror Their Energy

Match their pace, not yours. A calm person doesn't want your enthusiasm — they want your confidence. Read the room before you open your mouth.

🧠

Remember Details

Use their name. Reference something they said earlier in the conversation. Nothing says "I care" like showing you actually listened.

🤝

Find Real Common Ground

One genuine shared thing beats ten polished opening lines. People feel shared identity instantly — and it breaks the rep/prospect wall.

👂

Listen More Than You Talk

The prospect who talks most is the prospect who buys. Your job is to ask and listen — not to present. The presentation comes after they've sold themselves.

5-Step Objection Framework

Never freestyle it — run the process every single time

1
Hear Them Out Fully

Never interrupt. Let them finish completely. Silence after an objection is not weakness — it's respect, and it makes them feel heard.

2
Cushion It

"I understand how you feel — a lot of people I work with felt the same way at first." Empathy before argument, every time.

3
Clarify — Real or Smokescreen?

"Is that the main thing holding you back?" If yes, it's real. If they hesitate — it's a smokescreen. Find the real one.

4
Answer With Value, Not Defense

Don't argue. Don't justify. Show them the value that makes the objection irrelevant. Bridge to what they told you they actually want.

5
Confirm and Continue

"Does that make sense? So if we can handle that — are we good to move forward?" Close the loop, don't leave it open.

Group Vote — Real Objection or Stalling?
"Let me think about it and get back to you."
"We don't have budget approved for this quarter."
"I need to run this by my partner first."
"We're already using a competitor's product."
Teach the 5 steps, then run the group vote. For each objection, group shouts "Real" or "Smokescreen" — then click to reveal. Emphasize that smokescreens are the most common: they hide the real objection and kill the deal if you answer the wrong thing.

The Tie-Downs

Tie-Down

"Great product, isn't it?"

Appended to a statement to get a yes. Tag at the end — invites easy agreement and builds momentum.

Inverted Tie-Down

"Isn't it a great product?"

Leads with the question. Tag at the beginning — opens with empathy, prospect hears their pain reflected first.

Internal Tie-Down

"Because it's the best value, isn't it, you'd want to move forward?"

Embedded mid-sentence — keeps them nodding throughout a longer point. Harder to tune out.

Quick Quiz
Two of these are real tie-downs. One isn't. Click the one that's wrong.
"It's important to feel confident in your investment, isn't it?"
"This would save you time every single day, wouldn't it?"
"You'd be crazy not to take this deal, right?"
✗ "You'd be crazy not to..." is too aggressive. It creates pressure, not agreement. A tie-down should invite an easy "yes," not corner the prospect into one.
Look for the one that doesn't actually invite agreement.

The Close Openers

Alternative Option

"Would Tuesday or Thursday work better?"

Give two choices, both move forward. Never ask if — ask when and how.

Porcupine

"Which color appeals to you most?"

Answer a question with a question. "What color did you have in mind?" → bounce it back. Makes them reveal their own urgency.

Involvement Question

"Where would you put this in your office?"

Picture them owning it. Moves them from "should I buy" to "how do I use it" before you close.

3 Principles of Question Power

The rules that determine whether your questions move the deal forward or stall it.

🤝 Build the Bond First Always establish rapport before going for power questions.
🎯 Design for Desirable Answers Every question should move the conversation toward a sale — never a dead end.
🔄 Make the Decision First You can't lead a prospect to a decision you haven't already made yourself.

The Referral Script

Ask at peak satisfaction — immediately after the close, not days later

The Exact Phrase

"I'm really glad this worked out for you. Who's the one person you know who would benefit from the same experience?"

Don't Say

"Do you know anyone else who might be interested?"

Say This

"Who's the one person you know who would benefit from the same experience?"

Don't Say

"Feel free to pass along my number if anyone comes to mind."

Say This

"Can I reach out to them directly, or would you prefer to make the intro?"

💡 "Anyone who might be interested" requires them to search their whole network. "The one person" is specific — they picture a face immediately. One name is a warm lead. Vague is a dead end.
The timing is everything — ask immediately after the close while they're at peak satisfaction. Do not wait. Practice the exact phrase: "Who's the ONE person..." — the word "one" does all the work.

The Follow-Up Formula

A system beats willpower every time — run this process on every prospect

⚡
The 24-Hour Rule

Touch back within 24 hours of first contact, no exceptions. The longer you wait, the colder they get. Same-day is better than next-day.

🎯
Reference Something Specific

Never say "just checking in." Reference something they actually told you: "You mentioned the Q2 rollout — I wanted to share something relevant." It proves you listened.

🔁
The 3-Touch Sequence

Call → Email → Handwritten note (or personalized LinkedIn message). Each touch is a different medium. Don't just repeat the same channel three times.

🚪
Know When to Move On

After 5 touches with zero response — one final message: "I don't want to keep bothering you. If the timing changes, I'm here." Then move on. Respect earns callbacks.

The 3-touch sequence is the key concept. Most reps treat follow-up as one call. Top reps treat it as a multi-channel relationship. Ask the group: what's the biggest mistake people make in follow-up? (Answer: "just checking in" — it gives them nothing to respond to.)
